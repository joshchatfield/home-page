User Interface Consultant


what feeling do you want users to associate with your website?

You are not designing things for yourself, you are designing them for other people, keep them in mind. The user is looking for information; getting it easily will give a positive relaxed feeling, making them use effort will lead to frustration. This is the feeling the user will associate with your website. Give the user the most amount of information with the least amount of effort. Placement, organization, color, and size should all convey specific information. Do not limit yourself to verbose wording as the only way to communicate. In general, items should be large with ample spacing. Distractions are tiring, avoid them at all costs. Only present one set of information at a time.

In the eyes of the user beautiful is not a complicated or difficult task for the creator it is something done with them in mind. This is generally simple and to the point using all of the sensory information that the user will be taking in: color, size, placement, organization, timing (when things appear), movement, and sound.

Mobile Optimization


How something will appear on a mobile device can be tricky, unintuitive, and often overlooked, even by some of the biggest names in the business. If your competitor's site is clean, clear, and efficient on mobile and yours is not, customers will remember that.

Viewport Scalability
first big question is whether or not to use viewport scalability. This means when your content is viewed on a smaller screen do you want everything to shrink to scale with the container, leaving the positioning unchanged, or do you want the content to stay the same size as the container shrinks and let the positioning of everything shuffle around?

In the case of a site focused mainly on design and not bogged down with the responsibility of conveying a lot of information viewport may not be ideal. This is because there will definitely be movement of items if viewport scalability is turned on, and for something that was carefully laid out with design in mind you may not want that. In the case of an information heavy site, viewport is almost necessary. This is because on small devices writing that looks normal on larger screens will become illegibly small. Navigation Menu
Next big question is how to display the navigation menu, I have found that using a full page for this is most suitable. This preserves the majority of the screen for content because only a small button is necessary to give the user access to another screen which will serve as the menu.

Home Icon
Always have a button, preferably a logo/icon available to your user for them to return to the homepage, this should never leave their screen. Not only is this an opportunity to reinforce your brand but it is essential for user ease. A good example is the physical home button on the iPhone, how lost would you feel without it?

Device Width

The changing width of devices is the main variable to keep in mind while designing, and remember that most devices can be turned sideways.

Low Volume of Content

The screen is small so there should be few options per page at any one time. Remember that mobile browsers will auto show/hide menus of their own, even further reducing screen size. group things into larger categories if there are too many. This should be the case anyway, small screen or not, to give a clean sleek feeling that does not overwhelm the user.
